Inside the workflow 05
You write your document in your usual word processor or app, then paste the text into Grammarly's web app or desktop application. The tool immediately highlights issues across your writing — not just grammar and spelling, but repetitive words, jargon, and vague terms like 'great' that it suggests you replace with more descriptive alternatives. You click through each suggestion, accepting corrections for typos and considering word choice improvements. Grammarly explains why each change matters, so you learn patterns over time. For emails and daily business communication, you'd install the browser extension to get real-time checking as you type, though this means copying and pasting between programs when you want deeper analysis. The tool identifies homonyms, hackneyed phrases, and words non-native speakers commonly misuse, then offers specific replacements. You review each suggestion individually — some you accept immediately, others you reject because they don't match your intended tone or meaning.
  • —Real-time browser extension checking catches errors as you type, but PCMag's reviewer found the immediate feedback distracting and raised privacy concerns about a plugin reading everything written all the time
  • —Grammarly explains why changes are needed and helps users learn over time, but TrustRadius users report it doesn't learn individual writing styles or tone preferences — requiring manual override of suggestions that don't match personal voice
  • —Copy-paste method into Grammarly's app works well for documents and long-form content, but becomes inconvenient for emails and daily business communication where switching between programs costs time

Practical interpretation
Grammarly caught the subject-verb disagreement ('there's' to 'there are') and improved the email's structure by adding proper spacing and an exclamation point for a friendlier tone. However, it didn't flag the somewhat vague business jargon like 'touch base' or suggest more specific language about which areas need focus - improvements that would require the user's own editorial judgment.

Overview 07

Grammarly is an AI-powered writing assistant that checks grammar, spelling, punctuation, clarity, tone, and style across virtually every platform where you write—from Google Docs and Microsoft Word to email clients and social media. Founded in 2009, it has evolved from a basic grammar checker into what the company calls a comprehensive writing coach, now serving over 40 million users and 50,000 organizations worldwide.

The tool works by analyzing your text in real-time as you type, flagging errors and suggesting improvements through inline corrections and explanations. Beyond basic grammar fixes, Grammarly's 2026 version includes generative AI capabilities that provide 2,000 AI prompts per month in the Pro plan, allowing users to rewrite sentences, generate content, and adjust tone with one-click rewrites. The system integrates directly into your existing writing workflow through browser extensions and app integrations, so corrections appear wherever you're already working.

Grammarly's emphasis on real-time correction and cross-platform integration makes it most valuable for professionals, content creators, students, and non-native English speakers who write frequently across multiple platforms and need consistent quality control. However, it's less suited for casual writers who only occasionally write short messages, or those specifically looking for a full AI content generator rather than a writing improvement tool. The $12-30 monthly subscription cost reflects its positioning as a professional writing tool rather than a casual helper.
